<h5>Intro:</h5>

<p>
CSE 158 and 258 are undergraduate and graduate courses devoted to current methods for recommender systems, data mining, and predictive analytics. No previous background in machine learning is required, but all participants should be comfortable with programming (all example code will be in Python), and with basic optimization and linear algebra.
</p>

<h5>Lectures:</h5>

<p>
The course meets twice a week on Tuesday/Thursday mornings, starting September 26. The class meets in the Jeannie Auditorium, though meetings will also be livestreamed on twitch. Recordings will also be made available on this page after each class.
</p>

<h5>Assignment 2</h5>
<ul>
<li><b>Q:</b> Do we have to do better than existing methods?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> It's not compulsory that your method be "state-of-the-art", and certainly we understand that if you're trying a creative approach to a problem that it's not guaranteed to work. But, if your solution doesn't work well, you need to have a convincing explanation as to <em>why</em> it didn't work. You need to convince us that is was actually due to some fundamental challenges you encountered when modeling the data, and not merely due to a bug in your code. And before that you should convince us that your idea made sense and was <em>expected</em> to work in the first place!
<li><b>Q:</b> Can we use a different template than the one provided?<li>
<li><b>A:</b> Yes. The template is only intended as a length guideline, and even that is not strict.</li>
<li><b>Q:</b> Can we order the sections differently from what the spec says?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> You're welcome to do so, though you do have to mark the different components in gradescope. Each assignment will be graded by a single person, so we can usually sort through the different parts no matter what order they're in. Just help us make it easy to follow.</li>
<li><b>Q:</b> What are "baselines"? What baselines should I use?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> Baselines are comparison points that are intended to demonstrate (a) that your method works; and (b) what components of your method works. Baselines could include (1) simple techniques to validate that your method is working at all, e.g. trivial predictors (always predicting the mean, or most popular category, etc.), or predictors that use only a single feature; (2) state-of-the-art techniques, to show how well your method compares to the best existing solution; or (3) "ablation" methods, i.e., versions of your proposed model with a single feature/component removed; these demonstrate how much that feature was really useful when making predictions.</li>
<li><b>Q:</b> The spec says we should not use a dataset that is too small, but how small is too small? Is X too small?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> Again this is just a guideline. It's okay to use a small dataset <em>so long as you can apply the types of methods we've covered in class</em>. For example, you probably can't train a sentiment classifier with a large dictionary unless you have a dataset with tens of thousands of samples.</li>
<li><b>Q:</b> Is X dataset good for this assignment?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> Generally, what makes a dataset interesting for Assignment 2 is that it helps you to demonstrate a variety of concepts that were covered in class. I suggest datasets like Google Local/Yelp/etc. since they include a combination of temporal, linguistic, geographical, social, and recommendation data. Hence there are potentially <em>a lot</em> of techniques that could be used. Datasets like (e.g.) stock market trends are (generally) not so interesting as they include only limited features (possibly just sequences of numerical measurements), and mightn't make much use of the prediction techniques we saw in class.</li>
<li><b>Q:</b> Are datasets from Kaggle (or similar) okay?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> Maybe. Again, think about whether they really allow you to show off the different techniques we've covered in class. In some of the less interesting Kaggle datasets, you're essentially given numerical features to predict a given output variable. There's very little data processing or feature engineering to do (or it's done for you); there's very little problem specification or design (it's given to you). Hence the parts of the assignment that are the most interesting for others are already solved for you. If you want to pursue this route, you should expect to put <em>a lot</em> of work into producing a strong model, given that other components are more limited.</li>
</ul>

<h5>Homework</h5>
<ul>
<li><b>Q:</b> My homework is taking an extremely long time to run. Can we have access to any additional computational resources?</li>
<li><b>A:</b> I always make sure that homework questions (if implemented carefully) can be run in a short period of time on a low-powered machine. If your solution is running slowly, you should try to explore different data structures. It's often a case of (e.g.) applying a linear-time operation rather than a logarithmic one within a loop (such as using the "in" operator to a list rather than a set). These details become important for larger datasets. As for computing resources, I sometimes set up accounts on <a href="https://datahub.ucsd.edu/hub/login">datahub</a> or similar, though these don't offer much by way of power (and aren't necessarily easier than just running on your own laptop).</li>
</ul>

<li><b>Q:</b> Why are there no private posts on Piazza?</li>

</ul>
